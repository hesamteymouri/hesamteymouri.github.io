Methods

My research draws on a complementary set of empirical methods to study consumer judgment, technology-mediated experiences, and marketplace behavior.

← Back to Home

I use multiple methodological approaches to address research questions at the intersection of marketing, consumer behavior, and emerging technologies. Across projects, my goal is to pair the right method with the right theoretical question, whether that involves identifying causal mechanisms, measuring consumer preferences, or modeling complex and repeated observations.

Causal Inference

Behavioral Experiments

Behavioral experiments allow me to identify causal effects and test psychological mechanisms in controlled decision environments. I use experiments extensively to study how consumers respond to AI systems, service technologies, digital prompts, and marketplace design choices.

How I use this method
  • Testing how AI versus human decision-makers shape customer reactions
  • Examining service captivity, perceived control, and customer responses
  • Studying tipping prompts, donation design, and service interactions
Typical tools
  • Qualtrics, Prolific, and online between-subject designs
  • Mediation and moderation analysis
Modeling

Regression-Based Modeling

Regression-based modeling helps me evaluate theoretical relationships among variables and test explanations for consumer responses. I use these models to examine direct effects, mediation, moderation, and multi-variable relationships across behavioral datasets.

How I use this method
  • Testing theory-driven predictions in consumer and service research
  • Estimating mediating processes such as control, effort, and trust
  • Analyzing outcome variables such as attitudes, satisfaction, and behavioral intentions
Typical tools
  • R, SPSS, PROCESS, and generalized linear models
  • Cross-sectional and experimental data analysis
Quasi-Experimental

Difference-in-Differences (DiD)

Difference-in-differences models are useful when evaluating how outcomes change before and after an intervention relative to a comparison group. I use this approach when research questions require stronger causal identification in observational or panel data settings.

How I use this method
  • Assessing the impact of marketplace or platform changes
  • Studying policy or intervention effects on customer behavior
  • Comparing pre-post changes across treated and untreated groups
Typical tools
  • Panel regression, fixed effects, and robustness checks
  • R and Stata workflows for causal analysis
Preference Measurement

Conjoint Analysis

Conjoint analysis helps uncover how consumers make tradeoffs across product, service, and decision attributes. I use conjoint methods to study preferences when individuals must evaluate bundles of information rather than isolated variables.

How I use this method
  • Measuring consumer tradeoffs in service and technology contexts
  • Studying preferences for decision sources, features, and design attributes
  • Estimating the relative importance of competing offer components
Typical tools
  • Choice-based conjoint and utility estimation approaches
  • R-based analysis of stated preference data
Hierarchical Data

Mixed-Effects Modeling

Mixed-effects models are valuable when observations are nested, repeated, or clustered. I use these models when research designs involve repeated measures, individual-level heterogeneity, or grouped data structures that require more flexible estimation.

How I use this method
  • Analyzing repeated-measures and longitudinal observations
  • Modeling participant-level and group-level variation
  • Capturing heterogeneity in behavioral and customer-level data
Typical tools
  • R packages such as lme4 and related multilevel workflows
  • Experimental, panel, and nested data analysis
