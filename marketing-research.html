Marketing Research

Designing and using research to understand markets, measure customer insights, and support evidence-based marketing decisions.

Course Description

This course introduces students to the theory and practice of marketing research and its role in managerial decision making. Students learn how organizations use research to identify problems, evaluate opportunities, understand customers, and support strategic and tactical marketing decisions.

The course covers the full marketing research process, including problem formulation, exploratory research, secondary data analysis, qualitative and quantitative research design, measurement and scaling, questionnaire design, sampling, data collection, statistical analysis, and communication of research findings.

Emphasis is placed on the practical application of marketing research methods to real business problems. Students complete a semester-long research project in which they design and conduct a marketing research study from problem definition to final report. Basic statistical concepts commonly used in marketing research, including sampling distributions, normal distribution, hypothesis testing, correlation, and regression, are introduced with emphasis on interpretation and managerial relevance.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the role of marketing research in managerial decision making
  • Formulate marketing problems and translate them into researchable questions and objectives
  • Identify and evaluate appropriate sources of primary and secondary data
  • Design exploratory, descriptive, and causal research studies
  • Develop questionnaires and measurement scales that are reliable and valid
  • Apply sampling and data collection principles in a marketing context
  • Interpret basic statistical results and translate them into managerial insights
  • Communicate research findings clearly through written reports and presentations

What Students Learn

Problem Formulation

Students learn how to move from a broad managerial issue to a clearly defined research problem, research objectives, and actionable research questions.

Exploratory Research

Students use literature searches, focus groups, interviews, and other exploratory methods to better understand marketing problems before designing formal research.

Secondary Data Analysis

Students evaluate internal and external sources of data and learn how existing information can support managerial decisions and sharpen research design.

Survey & Questionnaire Design

Students design effective data collection instruments, write clear questions, reduce wording bias, and structure questionnaires for reliable measurement.

Measurement & Scaling

Students work with nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio scales and learn how reliability, validity, and measurement error affect research quality.

Sampling & Data Collection

Students learn how to define target populations, choose sampling methods, consider sample size, and understand sources of sampling and response error.

Marketing Data Analysis

Students prepare and analyze research data using summary statistics, visualizations, and basic inferential tools commonly used in marketing research.

Hypothesis Testing

Students are introduced to normal distribution, p-values, statistical significance, correlation, and regression with emphasis on interpretation rather than mathematical derivation.

Research Reporting

Students learn how to translate findings into clear managerial implications and present results professionally in both written reports and team presentations.

Applied Learning Approach

This course emphasizes hands-on, project-based learning. Throughout the semester, students work in teams on a marketing research project that mirrors how research is conducted in practice. Teams define a marketing problem, gather secondary information, develop a research design, create a questionnaire, collect data, analyze results, and present recommendations.

Individual assignments reinforce core skills such as secondary data search, experiment design, measurement, and question writing. Together, the individual and team components help students build both conceptual understanding and practical research ability.
